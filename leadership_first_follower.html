🤝 The First Follower

The move from writing the code to leading the people who write it. It looks like the next rung on the same ladder — it isn't. It's a different job you start as a beginner, and the instincts that made you a great engineer will quietly work against you.

First module of the Leadership track — the people half of Mobile Engineer → CTO. No runnable code here; instead, decision scenarios to pressure-test your instincts, and a builder for this module's deliverable: your own manager README.

1It's a career change, not a promotion

Becoming a manager is often framed as a reward for being a strong senior engineer. That framing is the trap. Management is a lateral move into a new profession — one where your years of hard-won engineering expertise no longer directly produce the outcome. You were an expert; now, on day one, you're a novice again.

The clue is in the name of this module. A leader with no followers is just a person with a title. Your authority now comes not from being the best coder in the room, but from people choosing to follow — because you make them more effective, not because the org chart says so.

2The maker's schedule vs the manager's schedule

Paul Graham's classic observation: makers and managers live on different clocks. A maker needs long, unbroken blocks — a single meeting at 2pm can wreck a whole afternoon of deep work. A manager's day is designed to be sliced into 30-minute units. When you switch roles, your calendar inverts, and mourning your lost maker time is part of the transition.

🛠️ Maker's day (old you)

09:00 — Deep work: feature
10:00 — …still in flow…
11:00 — …still in flow…
12:00 — Standup
13:00 — Deep work: feature
14:00 — …still in flow…
15:00 — …still in flow…
16:00 — Code review, ship

🧭 Manager's day (new you)

09:00 — 1:1 with Priya
09:30 — 1:1 with Sam
10:00 — Unblock, Slack, review
10:30 — Planning sync
12:00 — Standup
13:00 — Hiring debrief + notes
14:00 — Stakeholder 1:1
15:00 — Firefighting / context

Notice the manager's day has no two-hour block anywhere. If you keep committing to real coding tasks on the critical path, you'll do them at night and resent it. Which leads to the hardest lesson…

3Letting go of the code

Your leverage just inverted. As an IC, your impact was what you built. As a manager, your impact is what your team builds — and if you're on the critical path for delivery, you've become the bottleneck, not the multiplier.

The pull to "just do it myself" is enormous, because it's faster right now and it feels good — it's the one part of the job you're already expert at. But every time you take the interesting problem, you deny someone else the growth, and you make the team dependent on you. Staying a little bit technical (reading code, pairing, prototyping, keeping credibility) is healthy; owning shipping tasks is not.

The rule of thumb: if a task is on the critical path and only you can do it, the org is fragile and it's your job to fix that — by teaching, not by hoarding the task. Your new success metric is whether the team ships when you're on vacation.

4Your new definition of "done"

The job is no longer "close tickets." Your actual work now looks like:

  • Multiply — make each person more effective than they'd be without you (context, unblocking, removing friction).
  • Grow — develop people; a year from now they should be visibly more capable.
  • Direct — make sure the team is working on the right things, and knows why.
  • Shield & connect — absorb organizational noise; carry context in and results out.

These have long, invisible feedback loops. You'll end weeks unsure you did anything — no green squares, no merged PRs. Learning to feel productive from other people's output is the core identity shift of this role.

6The impostor phase is normal

You will feel like a fraud for a while — that's the expertise reversal, not a personal failing. You traded a domain you'd mastered for one you're just starting. Give yourself the same patience you'd give a new grad on your team: find a mentor or peer manager group, read widely (The Manager's Path, An Elegant Puzzle, High Output Management), and treat management as a craft you improve at deliberately — the Kaizen way.

7Deliverable — write your manager README

A "manager README" (or "user manual for working with me") is a short doc you share with your team: how you see your role, what you value, how you give feedback, how you run 1:1s. It shortcuts months of guesswork and models the vulnerability you want from them. Draft yours now — fill in what you can and generate a clean copy:

Your name / role (optional)
How I see my role as your manager
What I value in how we work
How I give — and want — feedback
How I run 1:1s
Communication & availability
My quirks / blind spots
Try it: (1) Work through all four scenarios — notice how often the "multiplier" answer means not doing the work yourself. (2) Fill in the README builder and generate it; share the real version with your team (or future team). (3) Do a one-week audit: how many hours did you spend making others effective vs doing IC work? That ratio is the truest signal of the transition.
